Discover the ancient mathematical formulas from the Vedas that make calculations faster and easier.

16 Vedic Sutras

These sutras form the basis of Vedic Mathematics

Ekadhikena Purvena

"By one more than the previous one" - This sutra is useful for squaring numbers ending with 5 and for decimal calculations.

Example: Squaring numbers ending with 5

To calculate 25²:

Multiply the first digit (2) by one more than itself (2+1=3), so 2×3=6, then append 25 → 625

Step-by-Step Process:

1
Take the number before 5 (in 25, it's 2)
2
Multiply it by one more than itself (2 × 3 = 6)
3
Append 25 to the result (625)

Nikhilam Navatashcaramam Dashatah

"All from 9 and the last from 10" - This sutra is used for subtraction from bases like 10, 100, 1000, etc.

Example: Subtract from 1000

To subtract 357 from 1000:

Subtract each digit from 9, and the last digit from 10: 9-3=6, 9-5=4, 10-7=3 → 643

Step-by-Step Process:

1
Write down the number to be subtracted
2
Subtract each digit from 9, except the last digit
3
Subtract the last digit from 10

Urdhva-Tiryagbyham

"Vertically and crosswise" - This is a general multiplication formula applicable to all cases of multiplication.

Example: Multiply 12 × 13

Multiply vertically: 2×3=6 (right digit)

Multiply crosswise and add: (1×3)+(2×1)=3+2=5 (middle digit)

Multiply vertically: 1×1=1 (left digit) → 156

Step-by-Step Process:

1
Multiply the right-hand digits (units place)
2
Multiply crosswise and add the results
3
Multiply the left-hand digits (tens place)

Paravartya Yojayet

"Transpose and apply" - This sutra is used for algebraic equations and division.

Example: Solve 2x + 5 = 13

Transpose 5 to the other side: 2x = 13 - 5 = 8

Then x = 8 ÷ 2 = 4

Step-by-Step Process:

1
Identify the term to be transposed
2
Change its sign and move it to the other side
3
Simplify and solve the equation

Shunyam Saamyasamuccaye

"When the sum is the same, that sum is zero" - This sutra is used for solving equations where the sum of the coefficients is zero.

Example: Solve (x+3)/(x+5) = (x+4)/(x+6)

The sum of numerators: (x+3)+(x+4) = 2x+7

The sum of denominators: (x+5)+(x+6) = 2x+11

Since these are not equal, this specific rule doesn't apply directly.

Step-by-Step Process:

1
Check if a common factor exists across all terms in an equation.
2
If so, that factor can be equated to zero.
3
Also applies when numerators are same, so denominator sum is zero.
